## Advanced Vocabulary

1. **Concise** (adjective) – giving a lot of information clearly in few words
   - *Example: "Keep your emails concise to respect the recipient's time."*

2. **Formal** (adjective) – following established conventions; official
   - *Example: "Use a more formal tone when writing to senior management."*

3. **Prompt** (adjective) – done without delay; immediate
   - *Example: "I appreciate your prompt response to my query."*

4. **Clarify** (verb) – to make clear or easier to understand
   - *Example: "Could you clarify what you mean by 'urgent'?"*

5. **Attach** (verb) – to fasten or join one thing to another
   - *Example: "I've attached the report you requested."*

6. **Follow-up** (noun/verb) – a continuation or repetition of something
   - *Example: "I'm writing to follow up on my previous email."*

7. **Regarding** (preposition) – concerning; about
   - *Example: "I'm writing regarding the meeting scheduled for Friday."*

8. **Elaborate** (verb) – to develop or present in detail
   - *Example: "Could you elaborate on the timeline for this project?"*

9. **Summarise** (verb) – to give a brief statement of the main points
   - *Example: "Let me summarise the key decisions from today's meeting."*

10. **Courtesy** (noun) – polite behavior; a polite gesture
    - *Example: "As a courtesy, I wanted to inform you of the change in advance."*

---

## British Expressions

- **"I trust this email finds you well"** – formal opening
- **"Further to our conversation"** – following up on a discussion
- **"Please find attached"** – the document is attached
- **"At your earliest convenience"** – when you have time (polite)
- **"I would be grateful if"** – polite request
- **"Many thanks in advance"** – thank you for future action
- **"I look forward to hearing from you"** – expecting a reply
- **"Please do not hesitate to contact me"** – feel free to get in touch
- **"Kind regards"** – professional email closing
- **"Just to keep you in the loop"** – to inform you
- **"Dropping you a line"** – sending a quick message
- **"Looping in"** – including someone in the conversation

## Extended Dialogue Practice

### Conversation 1: Discussing Email Etiquette

**Setting: 9:30 AM, Office desk area**

**Junior colleague (Amy):** Morning, David. Can I pick your brain about email etiquette?

**David:** Of course. What's on your mind?

**Amy:** I'm never quite sure how formal to be. When should I use "Dear" versus just the person's name?

**David:** Good question. I reckon if it's someone senior or external, "Dear" is safer. For colleagues you work with regularly, just their name is fine.

**Amy:** That makes sense. What about closings? I see people use everything from "Cheers" to "Kind regards."

**David:** It depends on the relationship and context. "Kind regards" is always appropriate. "Best regards" is slightly less formal. "Cheers" is quite casual – I'd only use that with people I know well.

**Amy:** Fair enough. And how concise should emails be? Mine tend to be quite long.

**David:** As a courtesy to the recipient, try to keep them brief. If you need to elaborate on complex points, consider a phone call instead.

**Amy:** That's a good shout. What about following up when someone hasn't replied?

**David:** I usually wait a few days, then send a polite follow-up. Something like "Just dropping you a line to follow up on my previous email."

**Amy:** And if I need a prompt response?

**David:** You can say "I'd be grateful for a response at your earliest convenience," but use that sparingly. Not everything is genuinely urgent.

**Amy:** Spot on. One more thing – when should I attach documents versus putting information in the email body?

**David:** If it's more than a paragraph or two, attach it. Always mention it clearly: "Please find attached the report you requested."

**Amy:** Brilliant. Thanks for clarifying all that, David.

**David:** No worries. Just to keep you in the loop, there's a good guide on the company intranet about email best practices.

**Amy:** I'll check that out. Cheers!

### Conversation 2: Reviewing a Draft Email

**Setting: 2:00 PM, Meeting room**

**Manager (Helen):** Thanks for bringing this draft to me before sending, Tom. Let's have a look.

**Tom:** I wanted to make sure the tone was right. It's quite a sensitive request.

**Helen:** Good thinking. Right, let's see... "I trust this email finds you well" – that's appropriately formal for the client.

**Tom:** I wasn't sure if that was too formal?

**Helen:** No, it's spot on for an initial email to a new client. Now, this second paragraph is quite long. Could you summarise it more concisely?

**Tom:** Fair point. I was trying to elaborate on all the details, but perhaps that's too much?

**Helen:** Exactly. Keep the email brief and offer to provide more detail if needed. You could say "I'd be happy to elaborate further if required."

**Tom:** That's more concise. What about the closing? I've put "Many thanks in advance."

**Helen:** That works, though it does assume they'll do what you've asked. Perhaps "I would be grateful if you could consider this request" is more diplomatic?

**Tom:** Good shout. And I should probably add "Please do not hesitate to contact me if you need any clarification."

**Helen:** Absolutely. That's a courtesy that shows you're available for questions. Have you attached the supporting documents?

**Tom:** Yes, and I've mentioned it: "Please find attached the proposal and timeline."

**Helen:** Perfect. One small thing – you've written "ASAP" here. In formal emails, I'd spell it out: "as soon as possible" or better yet, "at your earliest convenience."

**Tom:** Fair enough. I'll change that. So overall, the tone is appropriate?

**Helen:** Yes, it's professional and polite. Just tighten up that middle section and you're good to go.

**Tom:** Brilliant. Thanks for the feedback, Helen. I'll send the revised version to you before it goes out.

**Helen:** No need – I trust you've got it now. Just remember: formal but friendly, concise but complete.

**James:** I reckon that's a good idea. We could say "Further to the previous discussion, I wanted to clarify the next steps."

**Emma:** And be very specific about who's doing what and by when?

**James:** Absolutely. Make it concise and actionable. No one wants to read through a long email chain to figure out what they need to do.

**Emma:** What about all the people currently copied in? Should we keep them all in the loop?

**James:** I'd say only include people who genuinely need to be involved. As a courtesy, you could mention "I've streamlined the distribution list to those directly involved."

**Emma:** That's diplomatic. And for the tone – formal or more casual?

**James:** Given it's going to the supplier as well as internal people, I'd keep it professional. "Kind regards" rather than "Cheers."

**Emma:** Fair enough. Should I attach a summary document or put everything in the email?

**James:** If you can summarise it in a few bullet points, put it in the email. People are more likely to read it. You could attach a detailed timeline as supporting information.

**Emma:** Good thinking. I'll draft it now and send it to you for a quick review before it goes out.

**James:** Sounds good. And Emma – make sure you're clear about what response you need and by when. "I would be grateful for confirmation by Friday" is better than leaving it open-ended.

**Emma:** Spot on. Thanks for talking this through, James.

**James:** No worries. Prompt, clear communication saves everyone time in the long run.

## Essay 1: The Art of Professional Email Communication

**Word count: ~450 words**

Email has become the primary mode of professional communication, yet many people struggle to strike the right balance between being formal and being friendly, between being concise and being complete. In British business culture, email etiquette follows particular conventions that, whilst not rigid, are worth understanding.

The opening sets the tone for the entire email. "I trust this email finds you well" might seem overly formal to some, but it's a standard courtesy in British professional correspondence, particularly for initial contact or formal requests. For colleagues you email regularly, a simple "Hi [Name]" is perfectly acceptable. The key is matching your tone to the relationship and context.

Being concise is perhaps the most important principle of effective email communication. People receive dozens, sometimes hundreds, of emails daily. A rambling email that could have been three sentences is unlikely to receive a prompt response. I reckon the best approach is to state your purpose in the first sentence, provide necessary detail in a paragraph or two, and close with a clear call to action. If you need to elaborate extensively, consider whether a phone call or meeting might be more appropriate.

The phrase "please find attached" is quintessentially British business English. Whilst it might sound formal, it's the standard way to reference attachments. Similarly, "regarding" is preferred over "about" in formal correspondence. These small linguistic choices contribute to a professional tone.

Requesting action requires particular care. "I would be grateful if you could..." is more polite than "Please..." which in turn is better than "I need you to..." The phrase "at your earliest convenience" acknowledges that the recipient has other priorities whilst still indicating you'd like a reasonably prompt response. However, use it genuinely – not everything is urgent, and overusing this phrase diminishes its impact.

Following up on unanswered emails is a delicate matter. In British professional culture, there's often a reluctance to appear pushy. Waiting a few days before sending a polite follow-up is generally appropriate: "Just dropping you a line to follow up on my previous email regarding..." This acknowledges that the person might have missed your email or been too busy to respond, rather than assuming they're ignoring you.

The closing matters too. "Kind regards" is the safe, professional standard. "Best regards" is slightly less formal. "Warm regards" suggests a closer relationship. "Cheers" is casual and should be reserved for colleagues you know well. "Many thanks in advance" can be useful but assumes compliance, so use it carefully.

Ultimately, effective email communication is about clarity, courtesy, and consideration for the recipient's time. When you can summarise your message concisely, attach relevant documents, and make clear what response you need, you're demonstrating professional communication skills that colleagues and clients will appreciate.

## Essay 2: Common Email Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

**Word count: ~400 words**

Despite email being ubiquitous in professional life, certain mistakes appear repeatedly. Understanding these pitfalls can help you communicate more effectively and avoid misunderstandings.

The most common error is lack of clarity about what action is needed. Emails that ramble without a clear purpose leave recipients uncertain about whether they need to respond or do anything. I've learned to always include a specific call to action: "Could you please confirm by Friday?" or "I'd be grateful for your thoughts on this proposal." This courtesy to the recipient makes it clear what you need.

Another frequent mistake is inappropriate tone. Email lacks the nuance of face-to-face communication, so what seems neutral to you might read as curt or aggressive to the recipient. In British professional culture, erring on the side of politeness is generally safer. "I would appreciate..." is better received than "I need..." Even when following up on something overdue, maintaining a courteous tone is important.

The "reply all" epidemic is a particular bugbear in many organizations. Before hitting "reply all," ask yourself whether everyone on the thread genuinely needs your response. As a courtesy, you might say "Moving to direct email to avoid cluttering everyone's inbox" when taking a conversation one-to-one. Similarly, be thoughtful about who you're looping in – copying someone on every email doesn't keep them informed, it overwhelms them.

Attachment issues cause unnecessary confusion. Saying "please find attached" when you've forgotten to attach the document is embarrassing. I've developed the habit of attaching documents before writing the email, so I can't forget. Similarly, be specific about what you've attached: "Please find attached the Q3 report and budget summary" is clearer than "See attached."

Timing matters more than people realize. Sending emails late at night or over weekends can create an expectation of constant availability. Unless something is genuinely urgent, scheduling emails to send during business hours shows respect for work-life boundaries. Similarly, expecting a prompt response to an email sent at 11 PM on Friday is unreasonable.

Finally, many people fail to summarise long email threads. When a conversation has gone back and forth multiple times, taking a moment to clarify where things stand – "Further to our discussion, here are the agreed next steps" – saves everyone from rereading the entire thread.

Effective email communication isn't complicated, but it does require thought. Being concise, clear, and courteous will serve you well in virtually any professional context.
